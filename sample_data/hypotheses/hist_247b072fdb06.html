Top performers deliver most in crises when they also have authority and reach

In turnaround, restructuring, and other high-pressure settings, rare execution and stakeholder skills pay off most when strong contributors also sit at the center of cross-functional networks and have formal decision rights. Without both, their impact is more likely to stay localized, slowing resolution and concentrating work around them.

The hypothesis

In ambiguous, cross-functional stress contexts (e.g., turnaround, restructuring, or high-urgency delivery), individuals who score in the top tier on execution/commercial problem-solving and stakeholder influence will produce disproportionately better outcomes only when they also have both (a) high cross-functional network centrality and (b) formal decision rights. Among equally strong individual contributors, those lacking either authority or network access will show lower throughput, slower issue resolution, and greater dependency concentration than those with both.

Hidden mechanism

The mechanism is not simply that talent matters more when access exists; it is that scarce capability creates value only when it can be deployed through fast decision loops and distributed through others. Formal decision rights convert judgment into action, while cross-functional centrality converts expertise into coordinated execution and transfer. When either condition is missing, the person’s capability remains localized: others must route around them, decisions slow, and work piles up around a single node. The testable prediction is an interaction effect: the payoff from rare execution/commercial strength should be strongest when both network position and authority are high, weaker when only one is present, and weakest when both are absent.

Issues raised

  • The hypothesis is overloaded: it mixes assessment design, leadership archetypes, social network position, decision rights, and succession design into one claim. That makes it hard to test cleanly.
  • 'Rare, business-critical strengths' is vague and circular. You need operational definitions for rarity, criticality, execution, stakeholder influence, and commercial/problem-solving capability.
  • 'Usable under stress' is not defined. Stress could mean workload spikes, ambiguity, restructuring, turnaround, or time pressure. The mechanism may differ by context.
  • The claim assumes dense relationship centrality and clear decision rights are necessary moderators, but does not specify whether either one alone is sufficient, or which one matters more.
  • The 'single point of failure' framing is plausible but not yet proven. High individual contribution can be valuable without implying bottlenecked contribution; you need evidence of blocked throughput, dependency concentration, or slow decision cycles.
  • The hypothesis may be partly obvious to executives: of course scarce experts matter more when they have authority and networks. The novel part needs to be sharper than 'capability plus access equals impact'.
  • It is not yet clear what decision an executive would make differently after testing this. Hire differently? redesign roles? change succession? Without a specific decision, it risks becoming a commentary rather than a management lever.
  • 'Dense relationship centrality' sounds like network jargon unless translated into measurable indicators such as advice-network centrality, cross-functional ties, or escalation frequency.
  • There is an implicit causal story from network position and authority to value creation, but no clear timing. Does authority enable deployment of skill, or do high performers acquire authority because of prior performance? Reverse causality is a real risk.
  • The hypothesis may be confounded by role type: some jobs require centrality and authority by design, so the observed effect may simply reflect job architecture rather than a hidden mechanism.
  • 'Leadership-potential population' and 'no formal hierarchy' suggest the sample may not have enough outcome variation to test the claim robustly. Without actual role outcomes, the inference could be speculative.
  • The proposed intervention bundle is too broad. Development, decision-right redesign, backup coverage, and knowledge transfer are different levers with different owners and expected effects.
  • The narrative implies broad leadership assessments may over-reward polished behaviors, but the hypothesis itself does not directly test that concern. It shifts to utilization of rare skills, which is adjacent but not identical.
  • There is no explicit comparator group. Are we comparing rare-skill individuals with high centrality versus rare-skill individuals with low centrality, or versus non-rare-skill individuals?
  • The term 'resilient organizational capacity' is lofty but not operational. Capacity in what sense: revenue continuity, project throughput, customer retention, cycle time, or crisis response?

Suggested improvements

  • Split the claim into a testable interaction: rare critical skill predicts performance under stress more strongly when the person has high network centrality and formal authority.
  • Define each construct with measurable proxies: e.g., top-decile assessment scores for execution/commerciality, advice-network betweenness or degree centrality, formal decision-right level, and specific stress outcomes.
  • Specify the business outcome that matters: faster resolution of critical issues, reduced dependency concentration, higher project delivery under load, or improved turnaround performance.
  • Make the mechanism narrower: 'skill is trapped in the individual' is too broad. Test whether knowledge-transfer structures, escalation paths, or delegated authority mediate the relationship.
  • Add a clear falsifiable comparison: high-skill/high-centrality/high-authority individuals versus high-skill/low-centrality or high-skill/low-authority individuals.
  • Separate prediction from intervention. First test whether centrality and authority moderate the value of scarce skills; only then propose redesigning decision rights or backup coverage.
  • State the boundary condition: this may only apply in ambiguous, cross-functional, or crisis settings, not routine operations.
  • If the executive goal is succession planning, reframe toward 'identify roles where critical expertise is dangerously concentrated in one person and quantify the dependency risk.'
  • If the goal is talent deployment, reframe toward 'which archetypes should be placed in roles with formal authority and broad network access to convert skill into scalable impact?'
