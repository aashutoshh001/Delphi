Brokerage traits may predict faster advancement in roles that depend on coordination

The hypothesis

Among early-tenure employees in matrixed or high-dependency roles, SHL brokerage/influence scores will add incremental predictive power for 12–24 month promotion probability and compensation growth beyond level, function, geography, prior performance, and cognitive ability; in low-interdependence roles, the same scores will show little or no incremental lift. This should inform whether SHL brokerage traits are weighted more heavily for succession slates and high-mobility placements in roles that require cross-functional coordination.

Hidden mechanism

The mechanism is role-exposure matching. Brokerage-oriented traits create measurable value only when the job architecture forces repeated coordination across teams, decision rights, or stakeholder groups. In those roles, the person can convert influence capacity into faster information flow, better issue resolution, and more visible contribution, which increases the odds of pay growth and promotion. In low-interdependence roles, the same trait has fewer opportunities to create observable performance signals, so its incremental effect should collapse toward zero. To make this testable, define role interdependence using job-family dependency maps, span of internal stakeholders, cross-functional project load, or manager-rated coordination intensity, then validate the interaction out of sample against a simpler baseline model.

Issues raised

  • This is a near-duplicate of prior hypotheses: role-context moderation of psychometric signals and brokerage effects on compensation/promotion are already established in the record.
  • The main claim is unsurprising: network-oriented traits matter more in networked roles than in siloed roles. That is a generic interaction, not a sharp hypothesis.
  • It is not yet tied to a specific executive decision. Should the company redesign roles, change assessment cutoffs, alter succession slates, or prioritize certain placements?
  • The proposed mechanism is plausible but still surface-level unless you define measurable network exposure, interdependence, and bridge-point roles in operational terms.
  • The dependent variables are broad and partly conflated. Compensation growth, advancement, and business results are not the same outcome and may move on different timelines.
  • The cohort lacks hierarchy, 360s, and engagement data, so the hypothesis may be underidentified if you expect to infer “business value” from assessment profiles alone.
  • There is a substantial risk of post-hoc overfitting: brokerage-like competencies often correlate with generic managerial success, so any apparent effect could just be role seniority, visibility, or job family effects.
  • The hypothesis does not specify the baseline model, comparison group, or time horizon clearly enough for a downstream test.
  • It is unclear how SHL profiles would validly measure “cross-functional brokerage and influence” without constructing the feature in a way that is partly tautological.
  • The statement that isolated roles may mask value is testable, but as written it is too broad to reject or confirm cleanly.

Suggested improvements

  • Convert this into a single falsifiable interaction: e.g., brokerage score × role interdependence predicts pay growth over 12–24 months, controlling for level, function, geography, and prior performance.
  • Define bridge-point roles operationally using job architecture data, dependency maps, meeting/network traces, or manager-reported interdependence, not vague labels.
  • Split outcomes into one primary executive-relevant outcome and one secondary outcome. Example: promotion probability as primary, compensation growth as secondary.
  • State the business decision explicitly: should SHL scores be weighted differently by role family when selecting candidates for high-mobility or leadership tracks?
  • Add a null expectation and effect direction. For example, brokerage should have near-zero incremental lift in low-interdependence roles.
  • Pre-register the interaction and include an out-of-sample validation plan to avoid story-fitting.
  • If possible, compare against a simpler baseline such as general cognitive ability or prior performance to show incremental value, not just correlation.
  • Narrow the hypothesis to a specific cohort segment where the mechanism is most likely to operate, such as early-tenure employees in matrixed roles.
  • Operationalize business results in a way that a downstream analysis agent can actually test, such as revenue per head, project outcomes, or bonus progression.
  • Avoid claiming the assessment profile predicts both “business value” and “network position” unless you can separate trait measurement from role exposure; otherwise the mechanism is muddled.
