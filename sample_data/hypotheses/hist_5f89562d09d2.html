High-potential employees in low-stretch roles are more likely to quit

The hypothesis says that employees with strong leadership potential are more likely to leave when their jobs give them too little decision authority, complexity, or team scope. The risk appears highest when they have not had an internal move or promotion in the past year. For leaders, the implication is to spot and redesign underchallenging roles before top talent exits.

Organization: shl-sample-cohort · Generated: 2026-07-23T18:47:57.198935+00:00

The hypothesis

Among employees at the same level, function, geography, performance band, and tenure band, those in the top quartile of SHL leadership potential who are assigned to bottom-quartile job stretch (measured by a composite of decision-right scope, role complexity, and supervisory span) will have a higher probability of external attrition within the next 6–12 months than similarly rated peers in higher-stretch roles. This effect should be strongest among employees with no internal move or promotion in the prior year, indicating a retention risk driven by capability–role mismatch rather than mobility-driven exit.

Hidden mechanism

The mechanism is a specific capability–role misalignment on stretch, not a generic dissatisfaction story. High-potential employees are more likely to leave when their current job architecture underuses their assessed leadership capacity: they have little decision authority, limited complexity, and insufficient supervisory scope relative to what their SHL profile indicates they can handle. That mismatch creates a clearer exit signal than pay or tenure alone, because the employee can see a gap between their demonstrated capability and the developmental or advancement content of the role. By contrast, if a high-potential employee moves internally or is promoted, the same signal should not translate into undesirable attrition. This makes the testable business lever not broad manager matching, but targeted action for top-potential employees in low-stretch roles: retention review, accelerated placement, or redesign of the role to increase autonomy and scope.

Internal critique

Flags

Issues raised

  • The hypothesis is mostly a restatement of an intuitive idea: 'high-potential people leave when underutilized.' That is obvious and not especially novel.
  • The mechanism is plausible but underspecified. 'Higher relative to supervisory demands' is vague: relative to what metric, measured how, and on what scale?
  • It conflates several different mechanisms: mismatch, lack of autonomy, insufficient stretch, and advancement expectations. Those are not the same and would imply different interventions.
  • It does not cleanly separate attrition from internal mobility. A person leaving because they took a better internal role is not the same as undesirable external attrition.
  • The comparison set is weakly defined. 'Similarly paid, similarly tenured peers' is not enough; pay and tenure alone will not address level, function, geography, performance, manager, and labor market differences that drive attrition.
  • It may be too dependent on a latent construct from SHL scores that executives may not trust without strong validation against hard outcomes.
  • The phrase 'supervisory demands of their current role' is ambiguous: are you measuring manager span, autonomy, complexity, direct reports, decision rights, or team quality?
  • The hypothesis assumes high assessed leadership potential should create dissatisfaction in low-stretch roles, but that may just as plausibly correlate with promotion pressure or external marketability rather than role-person mismatch.
  • The proposed decision lever is broad and mushy. 'Internal placement and manager-match decisions' is not a concrete policy lever unless you specify threshold rules or a routing action.
  • It is highly similar to the prior hypotheses about fit, pay progression, and promotion differences driven by SHL profile and seniority.

Suggested improvements

  • Narrow the construct to one measurable mismatch dimension, such as autonomy, role complexity, or managerial span, rather than bundling multiple mechanisms together.
  • Define the exposure explicitly: e.g., an interaction between SHL leadership potential percentile and a quantified job-demand index built from level, scope, direct reports, decision rights, or role complexity.
  • State a sharper outcome window and outcome type: external attrition within 6-12 months, excluding internal moves and promotions if that is the business concern.
  • Add a stronger causal contrast or threshold: for example, compare high-potential employees in low-stretch roles versus high-stretch roles within the same level/function/geography and manager band.
  • Specify the business action: 'flag top-decile potential employees in bottom-quartile stretch roles for retention review or accelerated placement.'
  • Include a falsifiable prediction about heterogeneity, such as the effect being strongest for employees with recent promotion eligibility, strong external labor-market demand, or no recent internal mobility.
  • If you want distinctiveness from prior hypotheses, shift away from pay/reward and toward an explicit role architecture problem: whether mismatch between assessed capability and job design predicts exit even when compensation and tenure are controlled.
  • Use hard operational definitions for 'high potential' and 'supervisory demands' so a downstream agent can actually test it without inventing proxies.
