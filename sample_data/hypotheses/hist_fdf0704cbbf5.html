Cross-boundary brokers may advance faster in roles that depend on coordination

The hypothesis says employees who bridge groups in informal networks are more likely to see faster pay growth and promotion or high-potential nominations, even after accounting for prior performance and role context. The effect should be strongest in work that relies on cross-team coordination, and minimal in tightly structured, low-interdependence roles.

Organization: shl-sample-cohort · Generated: 2026-07-23T14:09:52.771939+00:00

The hypothesis

After controlling for prior performance, SHL/360/competency profiles, and assignment context, pre-outcome cross-boundary brokerage (measured as time-lagged betweenness or cross-cluster bridging in the informal collaboration network) will predict faster pay progression and higher probability of promotion/high-potential nomination, but primarily in roles or projects with high interdependence or weak formal coordination. Brokerage should add little or no predictive value in stable, low-coordination roles.

Hidden mechanism

The mechanism is not generic networking but coordination leverage: brokers reduce information frictions, translate across groups, and gain early access to decision-relevant problems, which increases their visibility and the perceived value of their contribution in settings where work depends on cross-boundary coordination. This effect should be separable from simple sponsorship or baseline talent only if brokerage is measured before the outcome window and tested net of role, manager, function, and prior performance. If the brokerage effect disappears in tightly structured roles or after those controls, the result would indicate that what looked like a brokerage advantage was really context or visibility; if it survives, it would show that network position creates incremental advancement value beyond individual assessment scores.

Flags

Issues raised

  • The hypothesis is directionally plausible but not surprising: cross-boundary brokers often get visibility, which can affect rewards. It needs stronger novelty than "networking helps careers."
  • It is too broad across outcomes. Faster pay progression, longer tenure, and higher HiPo designation are different mechanisms and may not move together; bundling them weakens testability and invites mixed results.
  • The mechanism is asserted, not isolated. Visibility, sponsorship, access to opportunities, and actual performance are all tangled together. This is not yet a clean test of brokerage versus general effectiveness.
  • The sample context is fragile: without formal hierarchy, role family, and business-unit context, network effects may just be proxies for unobserved role type or project assignment. That makes causal interpretation weak.
  • 'Informal collaboration network' is underspecified. If network construction is noisy or time-varying, the result may be an artifact of the measurement method rather than a real mechanism.
  • Matching on 'equally strong peers with similar SHL, 360, and competency profiles' is underpowered if those constructs overlap heavily. You risk comparing people who are still very different on unmeasured factors like function, manager quality, or project criticality.
  • HiPo designation is especially dangerous as an outcome because it may be endogenous to visibility and network position. If the talent process already rewards connectors, the hypothesis may simply rediscover calibration bias.
  • Longer tenure is ambiguous: brokers may stay because they are valued, or they may stay because their role is sticky. That does not necessarily imply personal contribution; it may reflect organizational lock-in.
  • The hypothesis would be stronger if it specified whether brokerage predicts advancement above and beyond formal performance, or whether it only matters in low-structure, high-coordination roles.
  • It is similar in spirit to the sponsorship/visibility prior hypothesis and partly overlaps with the cross-functional stress-context hypothesis because both mix network access with advancement or performance advantages. It may be redundant unless sharpened around informal brokerage specifically.
  • Executives would need an answer to 'what do we do differently?' Right now the implication is vague: retain integrators. That is too generic unless tied to specific talent actions or succession rules.
  • The hypothesized value mechanism could be reversed: high performers may become brokers because others seek them out, not the other way around. The current statement does not address directionality or temporal ordering.

Suggested improvements

  • Narrow the hypothesis to one primary outcome, preferably pay progression or promotion/HiPo designation, and treat tenure separately.
  • Define brokerage precisely: e.g., betweenness centrality or cross-cluster bridging measured on a pre-outcome time window.
  • Add a time-lagged design to establish ordering: network position at t0 predicting outcomes at t+12/t+24 months.
  • Control explicitly for role, function, manager, site, project type, and prior performance/visibility to reduce the chance that brokerage is just a proxy.
  • Test an interaction instead of a blanket main effect: brokerage should matter more in high-interdependence or low-formality roles than in stable, individual-contributor roles.
  • Separate visibility/sponsorship from brokerage by adding measures of nomination, manager endorsement, or talent-review exposure, so you can tell whether the effect is relational leverage or just attention.
  • If the executive action is to identify 'integrators,' define an operational threshold and a retention/promotion intervention, not just an interpretive label.
  • State a falsifiable alternative: brokerage should have no effect after controlling for prior performance and role context if advancement is purely merit-based; that would make the analysis sharper.
  • Consider reframing to ask whether brokerage moderates the payoff of SHL scores, rather than claiming it independently drives outcomes. That would better reveal a hidden mechanism and fit the existing battery.
  • If possible, segment by context where the network should matter most, such as matrix teams, cross-functional programs, or change-heavy assignments, to avoid a noisy average effect across the whole cohort.
