Opaque pay and promotion signals can drive top engineers to stall or leave

The hypothesis argues that even market-competitive compensation can backfire when high-performing engineers cannot see how pay changes and promotions connect to leadership readiness. That ambiguity can erode trust, raise flight risk among likely future leaders, and slow succession by weakening their willingness to invest in leadership behaviors.

Organization: acme-labs · Generated: 2026-07-23T09:40:47.006264+00:00

The hypothesis

High-performing engineers are more likely to become regretted attrition risks and stall in leadership progression when compensation changes and promotion outcomes are perceived as opaque or weakly linked to demonstrated leadership readiness, even if their absolute pay remains market-competitive.

Hidden mechanism

The hidden mechanism is fairness-based disengagement: employees who see rewards as inconsistently tied to the criteria that supposedly drive advancement may conclude that effort, readiness, and leadership capability do not reliably translate into recognition. That perception can reduce trust in the talent system, increase flight risk among the people most likely to be promoted, and slow succession because potential leaders either leave or stop investing in leadership behaviors. The effect should be strongest where performance is high but promotion and pay communication are ambiguous.

Issues raised

  • The claim is mostly a surface-level fairness story; nothing here is surprising. "Opaque comp/promotion leads to disengagement" is a generic organizational cliché, not a sharp hypothesis.
  • It overlaps heavily with the existing hypotheses on promotion readiness, promotion outcomes, and leadership progression. The novelty is low and it may be redundant rather than additive.
  • It bundles multiple constructs at once: high performance, regretted attrition, leadership progression, compensation changes, promotion outcomes, perceived opacity, and market competitiveness. That makes it hard to test cleanly and easy to explain away.
  • The mechanism is underspecified. "Perceived as opaque" is not directly observable unless you have survey/communications data, and you have not defined how to measure fairness perceptions robustly.
  • It does not clearly establish a causal path. Are people leaving because of compensation dissatisfaction, promotion disappointment, lower trust, or a general sense of stalled career growth? Those are different interventions.
  • The phrase "even if their absolute pay remains market-competitive" weakens the hypothesis by making it harder to action. If pay is already competitive, the proposed fix may not be compensation at all.
  • "High-performing engineers" is too broad. The effect likely depends on tenure, promotion eligibility, manager quality, or current level; without segmentation this will be noisy.
  • It does not reveal a hidden mechanism beyond a common mediation story: fairness perception -> disengagement -> attrition / slower leadership investment. That is plausible, but not hidden or especially diagnostic.
  • There is no clear threshold or comparison group. Without a specific contrast, a downstream analyst will struggle to test whether the effect is unique to opaque comp/promotion versus any dissatisfaction source.
  • The hypothesis mixes two outcomes that may not move together: regretted attrition risk and leadership progression stall. Those should be separated or linked with a clear sequence.

Suggested improvements

  • Narrow the hypothesis to one outcome and one mechanism. For example: "Among promotion-eligible engineers, opaque promotion communication predicts a higher 12-month regretted attrition hazard, especially for top-quartile performers."
  • Make the fairness mechanism measurable: use promotion cycle communication clarity, comp-change explanation quality, calibration transparency, or employee survey sentiment as the exposure.
  • Separate compensation from promotion. If you want both, define which one matters more and why, or test them as distinct predictors.
  • Add a boundary condition that makes it non-obvious, such as impact being strongest for employees with recent promotion denials, high manager turnover, or mismatch between self-rated and manager-rated readiness.
  • Translate the hypothesis into an intervention decision. Example: "If promotion-eligible high performers with low transparency scores show elevated attrition, Acme should redesign promotion communication before changing pay bands."
  • Use a more diagnostic comparison: compare high performers with similar pay but different promotion communication experiences, or compare teams with different calibration transparency.
  • If leadership bench is the concern, specify a measurable proxy such as future promotion rate to engineering manager, leadership competency ratings, or successor coverage, rather than the vague phrase "leadership progression."
