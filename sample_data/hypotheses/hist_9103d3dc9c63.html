Leadership scores predict promotions more than day-to-day performance

The hypothesis says baseline SHL leadership scores will forecast 12-month promotion speed and pay growth more strongly than same-level performance, especially in functions with high internal mobility and leadership-track progression. If true, the scores are less a measure of current effectiveness than a signal of who gets broader opportunities next.

Organization: shl-sample-cohort · Generated: 2026-07-23T18:31:01.534729+00:00

The hypothesis

Baseline SHL leadership competency scores will predict 12-month promotion velocity and compensation growth more strongly than they predict same-level performance, and that predictive value will be concentrated in roles and functions with high internal mobility and leadership-track progression. We expect the effect to be largest for top-decile leadership scores and to weaken materially in low-mobility roles, because the score is primarily a signal of advancement readiness rather than a universal proxy for on-role effectiveness.

Hidden mechanism

The proposed mechanism is opportunity mediation, but framed as a testable selection process rather than a hidden causal claim: stronger SHL results increase the likelihood that managers place people on promotion slates, assign broader scope, nominate them for stretch work, or move them into higher-exposure roles. Those opportunity gains are what then translate into faster pay and level progression. To avoid conflating this with prior performance, the design should be time-ordered: baseline SHL -> subsequent exposure proxies (lateral moves, scope changes, interim assignments, leadership-program nominations, promotion slate inclusion, manager changes) -> later promotion/compensation outcomes, with lagged controls for prior level and prior performance where available. The falsifiable test is whether SHL retains incremental predictive power after these exposure proxies are added: if it does, SHL is doing more than selecting people into opportunity; if it largely disappears, its value is mainly as an access-to-opportunity signal, especially in leadership-track roles.

Issues raised

  • The mechanism is plausible but not cleanly separable from selection bias: stretch assignments and promotions are usually themselves based on prior performance and manager judgment, so the hypothesis risks re-describing the same gatekeeping process rather than identifying an independent mechanism.
  • It may be impossible to distinguish 'assessment predicts opportunity' from 'assessment predicts performance that earns opportunity' without very strong time-ordering and exposure data.
  • 'Business outcomes' is too broad. Promotion velocity, compensation growth, and performance are different outcomes with different causal paths; bundling them weakens the hypothesis.
  • The claim could be confounded by role family and level: leadership scores may matter more in roles that are already on a leadership track, which makes the finding partly tautological.
  • The hypothesis is not very novel relative to the prior items. It is essentially another version of 'assessment scores work through manager-mediated opportunity and visibility,' just with different covariates.
  • The phrase 'once opportunity exposure is held constant' is doing too much work. If opportunity is measured crudely, controlling for it may induce bad adjustment or hide the real effect.
  • There is no explicit decision threshold or expected direction by subgroup. Without that, executives cannot tell whether to change assessment use, manager calibration, or succession policy.
  • The asset is described as weak on lived organizational context, yet this hypothesis requires exactly that missing context (stretch assignments, visibility, role complexity). It may not be testable at the claimed fidelity from the available data.
  • The hypothesis risks overclaiming causality from observational data. At best, you can test incremental predictive power and mediation patterns, not prove the hidden mechanism.
  • If leadership scores only predict access to opportunity, that may still be valuable; the hypothesis currently frames direct performance prediction as the baseline without clarifying why that distinction matters operationally.

Suggested improvements

  • Narrow the outcome to one primary business decision, such as promotion within 12 months or compensation progression, rather than 'business outcomes' generally.
  • Pre-register a time-ordered mediation design: SHL score at baseline -> subsequent assignment exposure -> later promotion/performance, with lagged controls for prior performance and level.
  • Specify measurable proxies for 'opportunity exposure' using data that likely exist: lateral moves, scope changes, manager changes, interim assignments, leadership program nominations, and promotion slates.
  • State the executive decision explicitly: for example, 'use SHL as an access-to-opportunity signal only in roles with high internal mobility, and downweight it for direct performance forecasting.'
  • Test heterogeneity by role criticality, function, and level instead of assuming a single global mechanism.
  • Separate two hypotheses: one about prediction of opportunity, one about prediction of post-opportunity performance, so each can be tested cleanly.
  • Add a falsifiable contrast: if SHL remains predictive after accounting for opportunity exposure, the mechanism is not just mediation; if it disappears, the score is mostly a selection/access tool.
  • Define whether 'leadership competency' means a composite, specific UCF dimensions, or top-decile scores. Executives need to know which construct matters.
  • Avoid saying 'hidden mechanism' unless you can actually observe the mediator. Rename it as 'hypothesized opportunity mediation' to reduce overstatement.
