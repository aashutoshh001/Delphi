Promotion becomes less tied to rewards as roles get more senior

The hypothesis argues that strong ratings, bonuses, or pay growth may predict promotion at lower levels, but matter less as employees move up. At higher levels, advancement depends more on evidence of strategic judgment, learning speed, and influence. For executives, that suggests promotion systems may be rewarding current performance while selecting future leaders by a different standard.

Organization: shl-sample-cohort · Generated: 2026-07-23T18:33:24.388673+00:00

The hypothesis

Controlling for job level, function, tenure, manager, prior promotion opportunity, and formal performance-rating criteria, employees with higher short-term reward signals (performance rating, bonus/merit growth, or pay progression) will not be equally likely to be promoted if their SHL UCF profile is concentrated in execution-oriented competencies rather than strategic-adaptation competencies (e.g., strategic thinking, learning agility, influence/impact). Specifically, the predictive gap between reward and promotion will widen with seniority: at higher job levels, promotion will depend more on strategic-adaptation scores than on contemporaneous reward signals, and this divergence will be strongest in functions or geographies with greater role complexity or smaller promotion pools.

Hidden mechanism

The hidden mechanism is not merely that senior roles require 'more leadership,' but that organizations implicitly switch the decision rule as employees move up the hierarchy. Near the middle levels, reward systems primarily reinforce visible current performance, while promotion decisions increasingly weight evidence of cross-boundary influence, learning speed, and readiness for ambiguity. This creates a measurable decoupling between being rewarded for execution and being selected for advancement: the same employee profile can continue to earn strong ratings or compensation growth yet fall short on the specific competency mix used to justify upward movement. The critical test is the level-by-level interaction: if the reward-to-promotion relationship weakens as strategic-adaptation competencies become more decisive, that indicates a real shift in merit criteria rather than a generic performance-promotion gap. If the gap is absent after controlling for promotion opportunity and role constraints, the hypothesis is falsified; if it is concentrated in particular functions or strata, that identifies where the hidden filter is operating and what promotion architecture or calibration policy should be changed.

Issues raised

  • The core claim is close to obvious: high performance does not always translate to promotion, and higher roles often emphasize broader leadership traits. That is standard HR doctrine, not a sharp hypothesis.
  • The hypothesis is under-specified. 'Strong current performance signals,' 'weaker strategic-adaptation and leadership-potential competencies,' and 'larger at higher job levels' are too vague to test cleanly without explicit measurement definitions and thresholds.
  • It conflates two different outcomes: reward in the present versus promotion later. Those may move together, separate, or be driven by different processes; the hypothesis should specify which proxy outcome is expected to diverge and by how much.
  • The proposed mechanism is plausible but not hidden enough. A 'second-stage filter' for leadership readiness is exactly what many promotion systems are designed to do. Saying organizations use broader leadership criteria is not revealing a novel mechanism.
  • The hypothesis risks tautology: if leadership-potential competencies are part of promotion criteria, then of course low scores on them predict lower promotion. The interesting part would be whether the gap widens after controlling for formal promotion criteria and role-level constraints.
  • It does not clearly identify the decision point executives would act on. Knowing there is a reward-to-promotion gap does not tell them whether to change calibration, redesign promotion criteria, retrain managers, or alter leveling architecture.
  • The comparison across job levels may be confounded by changing candidate pools, smaller promotion opportunities, different pay systems, and eligibility rules. A widening gap might reflect structural bottlenecks rather than a different merit model.
  • It is similar in spirit to the recorded hypotheses about sponsorship, HiPo status, and succession-pool attrition: all are about hidden filters beyond performance that affect advancement.
  • The available outcomes named in the narrative are proxies, but the hypothesis still assumes promotion is observed well enough to compare against rewards. If promotion data are weak or inconsistent, the test may be noisy or biased.
  • The hypothesis does not propose a clear falsifier. If the gap is flat across levels, is that because the mechanism is false, because competencies are mismeasured, or because promotion processes are uniform?
  • The phrase 'rewarded in the present' is ambiguous: pay, bonus, performance rating, assignment quality, visibility, or recognition? Each would imply a different mechanism and test.

Suggested improvements

  • Reframe into a sharper, falsifiable question: 'After controlling for level, function, tenure, manager, and prior promotion opportunity, does performance-based reward predict promotion less strongly than leadership-potential competency, and does that differential increase at higher levels?'
  • Define the exact reward outcome and promotion outcome separately, using observable proxies such as performance rating, bonus, merit increase, pay growth, job-level increase, or successor nomination.
  • Specify the competency dimensions operationally, preferably from SHL UCF scores, and pre-register a small set of constructs such as strategic adaptability, learning agility, and influence.
  • Add a mechanism test: compare level-by-level marginal effects of performance versus leadership-potential scores on promotion, rather than just comparing raw promotion rates.
  • Make the hypothesis decision-relevant by tying it to a policy choice, e.g., whether to modify promotion calibration criteria, build level-specific competency weights, or separate compensation from promotion more explicitly.
  • If possible, include a benchmark against role complexity or job family so the analysis can distinguish true readiness filtering from structural promotion scarcity.
  • Strengthen novelty by testing whether the reward-to-promotion gap is concentrated in certain functions, geographies, or manager strata, which would reveal where the hidden filter actually operates.
  • State a clear null hypothesis and expected direction with an explicit interaction term for job level.
